NASA Neutral Buoyancy Laboratory

Explore how astronauts train underwater for space missions

Divers

Highly trained safety divers work alongside astronauts to guide them, monitor safety, and provide real-time support during underwater spacewalk simulations. The NBL employs both SCUBA and surface-supplied diving systems, with nitrox and air, ensuring comprehensive support for astronauts during training sessions.

Neutral Buoyancy

The massive pool creates near-weightlessness, allowing astronauts to practice assembly, repairs, and complex space tasks as if they were in orbit. The NBL has extensive experience manipulating buoyancy to simulate full or partial gravity, facilitating realistic training scenarios for spacewalks.

Site Support

Engineers and mission specialists coordinate from control rooms, tracking astronaut movements and giving precise instructions during each simulation. The NBL is equipped with multiple integrated control rooms, extensive video, audio, and instrumentation capabilities, ensuring seamless communication and support during training operations.

Life Support & Facilities

From air systems to suit operations, the facility ensures astronaut health and safety while maintaining realistic mission conditions. The NBL includes an on-site hyperbaric chamber, hypobaric altitude chamber, medical treatment room, and locker room facilities, supporting comprehensive life support during training sessions.

History & Construction

Built in the 1990s, the NBL repurposed an industrial building near Johnson Space Center into the world’s largest astronaut training pool. The first suited dives took place in 1996, and in 1997 it was named the Sonny Carter Training Facility in honor of astronaut Manley “Sonny” Carter.

EVA Testing & Future Use

The NBL is also used to test tools, hardware, and spacewalk procedures before real missions. It now supports training for Artemis, commercial spaceflight, and next-generation EVA suits. Over 150 EVAs have been trained at the NBL, including missions to the International Space Station and Hubble Space Telescope.
