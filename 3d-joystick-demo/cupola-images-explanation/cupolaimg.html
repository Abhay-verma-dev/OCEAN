Cupola Observation Module

Explore breathtaking Earth views and ISS operations from the Cupola

Inside the Cupola

The Cupola provides astronauts with a panoramic view of Earth and space through its seven windows, making it the perfect place for observation and photography.

Equipped with a robotic workstation, the Cupola allows astronauts to control the Canadarm2 robotic arm, facilitating tasks such as spacecraft docking and external maintenance operations. This functionality is crucial for the International Space Station's (ISS) operations and maintenance.

Earth Observation

Astronauts capture stunning images of auroras, storms, and geographic landmarks to support science and outreach.

The Cupola's unique positioning and design enable astronauts to observe and document Earth's dynamic weather systems, natural disasters, and other phenomena, contributing to scientific research and public engagement.

Robotics & Operations

The Cupola houses the robotics workstation for controlling Canadarm2, assisting with docking, external experiments, and spacewalks.

This operational capability is vital for the assembly and maintenance of the ISS, allowing for precise manipulation of equipment and modules in space.

Night Views

At night, astronauts watch city lights, thunderstorms, and auroras — some of the most spectacular views aboard the ISS.

The Cupola's large windows provide an unobstructed view of Earth's night side, offering a unique perspective of human civilization and natural phenomena under artificial lighting.

Geographic Landmarks

From the Horn of Africa to the Himalayas, crew members photograph Earth’s unique landscapes through the Cupola windows.

These images serve as valuable resources for geographical studies, environmental monitoring, and educational purposes, showcasing Earth's diverse terrains and ecosystems.

History & Purpose

The Cupola module, built by the European Space Agency, was launched aboard Space Shuttle Endeavour in February 2010. Its seven windows provide panoramic views of Earth and serve as the primary control station for robotic operations aboard the International Space Station.

Installed on February 15, 2010, the Cupola has become an iconic feature of the ISS, symbolizing international collaboration and the human desire to explore and understand our planet from space.
